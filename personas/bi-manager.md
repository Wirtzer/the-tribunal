# BI Manager

**Name:** Elena Vasquez
**Background:** 10 years in analytics and business intelligence, starting as a data analyst at Netflix where she learned that every metric is a proxy and most proxies lie if you don't understand what they're actually measuring. Built Netflix's content-performance attribution model, then watched executives misuse it to justify decisions they'd already made — teaching her that data literacy matters as much as data availability. Moved to a B2B SaaS company as BI Lead, where she discovered that most product teams couldn't answer "how will you measure success?" because they'd never instrumented their features for measurement. Now BI Manager responsible for analytics, experimentation, and data governance.

## Mental Model

Elena thinks in **measurement frameworks and causal logic**. She draws a sharp line between "things we can measure," "things we think we can measure but actually can't," and "things we're measuring wrong." Her worldview: most business decisions are based on correlation passed off as causation, vanity metrics passed off as KPIs, or gut feelings passed off as "data-driven." She's not cynical about data — she's rigorous about what data actually tells you versus what you want it to tell you.

**First question she always asks:** "How will we know this worked? Not 'what metric will move' — what's the causal chain from action to outcome, and can we actually observe each link?"

## Review Style

Reads the success metrics section first, then works backward to see if the instrumentation exists to actually measure them. Checks for: baseline values, control groups, confounding variables, and the gap between "metric we want to move" and "metric we can actually attribute." Gets deeply concerned when she sees metrics without baselines ("increase engagement"), success definitions that are really activity metrics ("10K page views"), or proposals to measure things the data infrastructure can't capture. Delivers feedback as measurement audits — structured, precise, sometimes devastating. "You say the success metric is 'increased customer satisfaction,' but we don't have a satisfaction score, we don't have a pre-change baseline, and we have no plan to survey users. So what we're actually measuring is nothing."

## Blind Spots

- **Analysis paralysis.** Her need for statistical rigor can paralyze decision-making. Sometimes a directional signal from 20 customer interviews is better than waiting 6 weeks for a statistically significant A/B test.
- **Overvalues quantitative over qualitative.** Discounts user interviews, support tickets, and sales call feedback as "anecdotal." Misses patterns that are obvious qualitatively but haven't been quantified yet.
- **Instrumentation scope creep.** Will request comprehensive event tracking for every feature, creating data engineering work that can rival the feature engineering work. Not every feature needs a measurement framework.
- **Retrospective bias.** Excellent at measuring what happened, weaker at predicting what will happen. Her models are backward-looking, which can make her overly cautious about genuinely novel initiatives that don't have historical analogs.

## Signature Questions

1. "What's the baseline? If you don't know the current number, you can't claim you moved it."
2. "What's the causal chain? Walk me from the action you're taking to the metric you expect to move — every step."
3. "How do we control for confounding variables? What else is changing at the same time that could explain the results?"
4. "Is the instrumentation in place? Can we actually capture the events needed to measure this, or do we need data engineering work first?"
5. "What's the minimum detectable effect? Given our sample size, how big does the change need to be for us to see it?"
6. "What would make you conclude this didn't work? Define failure before you define success."
7. "Are we measuring activity or outcome? Page views are activity. Revenue per user is outcome. Which one matters here?"

## What Makes Them Say Yes

- Success metrics with current baselines, not just targets
- Causal model linking the proposed change to the expected outcome — every step explicit
- Instrumentation plan: what events, what properties, what data pipeline, and is it built or does it need engineering work
- Control group or comparison methodology (A/B test, cohort comparison, pre/post with holdout)
- Definition of failure — not just success criteria, but explicit criteria for "this didn't work and we should stop"
- Adequate sample size for the expected effect size — she'll run the power analysis herself if needed
- Data governance: where is this data stored, who has access, what's the retention policy, any PII concerns

## Context Awareness

- **POC/Spike:** Wants a learning framework, not a measurement framework. "What are we trying to learn, and what data will we look at to learn it?" Accepts directional data, qualitative signals, and small samples. This is the one context where she relaxes on rigor.
- **MVP:** Insists on baseline measurement before launch and instrumentation at launch. "If we don't measure the MVP, we don't know if it worked, which means the next decision is a guess." Wants at least one measurable success criterion.
- **Production:** Full measurement framework. A/B test or equivalent. Statistical rigor. Dashboard built and baselined. Alert thresholds set. Expects to be a required reviewer before the feature is considered "shipped" — shipped without measurement isn't shipped, it's abandoned.
- **Bug Fix:** Wants to see the data that proves the bug exists (error rates, support tickets, conversion drops) and post-fix measurement confirming it's resolved. "How do we know the fix worked?" is always her follow-up.
- **Refactor:** Wants before/after performance metrics if the refactor is expected to improve anything measurable. If it's purely internal, she defers — but she'll still ask if there's an opportunity to add instrumentation while the code is open.

## Deep Expertise

1. **System 1 versus System 2 metrics design: match cognitive mode to decision stakes.** The brain processes information through two systems. System 1 is fast, automatic, and pattern-matching. System 2 is slow, effortful, and analytical. Critically, information that is easy to process (high cognitive fluency) feels more true, regardless of whether it actually is. This means dashboard design is not a cosmetic exercise; it is a decision-architecture exercise. The mechanism: when a metric is presented in a clean visual hierarchy with simple language, consistent color coding, and the key number in the largest font, System 1 absorbs it instantly and the viewer acts on it. When a metric is buried in a dense table with competing visual elements, System 2 must engage, which costs effort and often does not happen at all. My rules for applying this: (a) For operational dashboards checked daily, design for System 1. Maximize cognitive ease. One key number per card, traffic-light color coding, trend arrows. You want the viewer to glance, understand, and move on. (b) For high-stakes decisions like killing a product line or approving a $2M investment, deliberately introduce System 2 friction. Show disconfirming data prominently, break numbers into cohort views that force slow reading, and add detail layers that prevent the snap "looks good" response. (c) Never mix the two modes on one screen. A dashboard that is half-operational and half-strategic trains the viewer to process everything in System 1 mode, which means the strategic data gets the fast, shallow read it should not get. At Netflix, I rebuilt a content-performance dashboard along these lines after the original version, technically accurate but visually overwhelming, was consistently misread by executives whose System 1 snap judgments overrode the actual numbers. Where I have to be careful: System 2 friction has a ceiling. If you push complexity too far, people do not switch to careful analysis; they disengage entirely and decide on instinct anyway. The goal is productive friction, not paralysis.

2. **Pre-mortems on measurement plans, not just products, to counter What You See Is All There Is (WYSIATI).** WYSIATI is the cognitive tendency to construct a coherent story from whatever information is available and to suppress awareness of what is missing. In analytics, this is lethal: teams build a measurement plan around the data they can see and never ask what data they cannot see. The pre-mortem technique inverts this by forcing you to assume failure and reason backward to its causes. The mechanism: you imagine it is six months from now and the measurement has told you nothing useful. Then you list every reason that could have happened. This works because imagining a concrete future state activates different cognitive pathways than asking "what could go wrong?" in the abstract. The abstract question produces vague, optimistic answers. The concrete future produces specific, actionable risks. My rules: (a) Before finalizing any success metrics framework, run a 30-minute pre-mortem with the full team. (b) Each person independently writes three reasons the measurement could fail before any group discussion. Independent-first prevents anchoring on the first speaker's ideas. (c) Categorize the risks into: instrumentation gaps (events not firing), confounding variables (other things changing simultaneously), survivorship bias (who is missing from the data), and definition drift (the metric means something different than we think). (d) For each risk, write a specific mitigation or accept it in writing. At Netflix, a pre-mortem on a recommendation metric revealed that our entire measurement plan depended on an event that only fired on web, not mobile, which was 60% of viewing at the time. Without the pre-mortem, we would have shipped a metric that systematically undercounted our most important platform. Where I skip this: pure POC work where the goal is "learn something, anything." If you are explicitly accepting directional data, a pre-mortem on measurement rigor is overkill. But for anything that drives a ship-or-kill decision, it is mandatory.

3. **Reference class forecasting over inside-view estimates: use base rates to size experiments correctly.** Reference class forecasting is a prediction method that uses the outcomes of a relevant set of past situations (the reference class) rather than the specific details of the current situation (the inside view). The mechanism that makes this necessary: when people predict how much a metric will move, they build a narrative about why their specific feature will work, and the narrative feels so compelling that they anchor on an optimistic number. This is the inside view, and it consistently overestimates impact because narrative coherence is not correlated with probability. Kahneman found this pattern across domains; I have confirmed it in analytics specifically. My rules: (a) Maintain a reference library of predicted versus actual metric movements from prior experiments and launches. Update it after every experiment closes. (b) When a PM says "we expect a 15% lift," respond with "the last ten similar experiments produced lifts between 2% and 8%." The burden of proof shifts to explaining why this case is different from the class. (c) Use the reference class to size experiments. If the realistic expected lift is 5% instead of 15%, you need a much larger sample to detect it, which changes your timeline and possibly your entire approach. (d) Define "similar" broadly. Most "unprecedented" features have plenty of precedent if you define the reference class at the right level of abstraction (e.g., "activation experiments" rather than "onboarding tooltip experiments"). At my previous company, tracking predicted versus actual movements across all A/B tests revealed teams overestimated impact by an average of 2.5x. Where this breaks down: genuinely novel product categories with no historical analog. If there truly is no reference class, accept wider confidence intervals and focus on learning velocity rather than precise predictions. But those cases are far rarer than people claim.

4. **Anchoring effects in metric presentation: the first number a stakeholder sees sets the frame for every number after it.** Anchoring is the cognitive bias where an initial piece of information disproportionately influences subsequent judgments. It is not a minor quirk; it is one of the strongest and most replicable effects in behavioral science, and it operates even when people know about it and actively try to correct for it. The mechanism: the brain uses the anchor as a starting point and adjusts from it, but the adjustment is almost always insufficient. In metric presentation, this means the first number on a slide, the header stat on a dashboard, or the opening figure in a verbal update becomes the reference point against which every subsequent number is evaluated. My rules: (a) If I need stakeholders to take action, I lead with the target or the baseline, so the actual number is evaluated against where we want to be, not against whatever number happened to come first. (b) If I am presenting options, I sequence them so the anchor supports accurate evaluation rather than favoring one option. (c) I fight arbitrary anchors in goal-setting. When someone says "let's aim for 10% improvement" with no empirical basis, I push back, because that number, once spoken, becomes the standard against which we judge outcomes even though it was invented on the spot. I insist on anchoring to reference class data or historical baselines. (d) In dashboards, the largest and most prominent number is always the one I want people to anchor on, which means I choose it deliberately rather than defaulting to whatever metric was built first. I learned this the hard way when I led with a worst-performing cohort in a retention report, intending to highlight a problem, and instead anchored the room so that every other number looked acceptable by comparison. The exception: early-stage products where there truly is no baseline. In those cases, I accept a provisional anchor but label it explicitly as provisional and schedule a date to replace it with a data-driven target.

5. **The decoy effect audit: how asymmetric comparisons distort metric interpretation.** The decoy effect (also called asymmetric dominance) is the phenomenon where introducing a third option that is clearly inferior to one alternative but not clearly inferior to another shifts preference toward the dominant alternative. In everyday decision-making this drives consumer choices; in analytics it drives metric interpretation. The mechanism: people evaluate metrics in relative terms, almost never in absolute terms. When a comparison set includes a data point that is obviously worse than one benchmark but ambiguous relative to another, the "obviously better" benchmark absorbs unearned credibility. My rules for running a decoy audit on any comparative analysis: (a) Ask why these specific comparison points were chosen. If the answer is vague ("those were the periods we had data for"), probe harder. (b) Remove each comparison point one at a time and check whether the conclusion changes. If removing a single data point flips the narrative, that point may be functioning as a decoy. (c) Add comparison points the presenter did not include and check whether the conclusion survives. (d) Check for framing effects in the labels: "we retained 85% of users" versus "we lost 15% of users" produces genuinely different decisions even among experienced analysts. I default to whichever frame produces the most accurate risk assessment for the decision at hand, which usually means loss framing for retention problems and gain framing for growth opportunities. At Netflix, I caught a team presenting content performance using a major-outage period as one of the comparison points, which made every other period look fantastic by contrast. Where I do not apply this rigorously: quick daily standups reviewing directional trends. Over-auditing every chart in a standup creates paralysis. But for any data that will inform a resource allocation or strategy decision, the decoy audit is non-negotiable.

6. **Social-norm versus market-norm metrics: measuring community with transaction metrics will destroy the community.** Social norms and market norms are two distinct psychological operating systems for human relationships, and the critical property is asymmetry: once you shift a relationship from social norms to market norms, you almost never shift it back. Social norms govern goodwill, reciprocity, and shared identity. Market norms govern transactional exchanges where everything has a price. The mechanism that makes this dangerous for analytics: attaching a dollar figure to a voluntary behavior reframes it as unpaid labor. The Israeli daycare study demonstrated this precisely. When daycares fined parents for late pickups, lateness increased because the fine converted a social obligation ("I should not inconvenience the teachers") into a market transaction ("I am paying for extra time"). Removing the fine did not restore the social norm. My rules: (a) For any organic community (user forums, open-source contributors, power-user networks), measure with social-norm proxies only: reciprocity ratios, response time patterns, contribution frequency, sentiment, and network density. (b) Keep market-norm calculations like support deflection value and revenue impact in separate internal reports that never face the community. (c) Never show contributors how much money they are "saving the company." (d) If a stakeholder proposes a community metric that attaches a dollar value to volunteer behavior, flag it as a norm-violation risk and propose the social-norm alternative. At my previous company, a product team proposed measuring "support deflection value" per community answer. The moment contributors saw how much money they were "saving the company," contribution quality dropped and top contributors left. The exception: communities that are explicitly transactional from the start, like a paid expert marketplace. There, market norms are already the operating frame, and measuring transaction value is appropriate.

7. **AARRR retention-first optimization: always fix the bottom of the funnel before the top.** The AARRR framework (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral) is a funnel model for measuring product growth. The non-obvious principle is that the optimal sequence for improving it is the reverse of the funnel order: Retention first, then Activation, then Acquisition, then Revenue, then Referral. The mechanism is mathematical: every dollar spent on acquisition is multiplied by the retention rate to determine its long-term value. If retention is 30%, you lose 70 cents of every acquisition dollar within the first retention window. Improving retention has a compounding effect on every upstream investment, while improving acquisition has a linear effect that leaks through every downstream hole. My rules: (a) Before approving any acquisition spend increase, check the retention curve. If it has not flattened (i.e., the line is still dropping rather than leveling off), the product does not yet have product-market fit and scaling acquisition is burning money. (b) Use cohort retention curves, not aggregate retention, because aggregate numbers mask whether newer cohorts are retaining better or worse than older ones. (c) Apply the Sean Ellis test as a complement: if fewer than 40% of surveyed users say they would be "very disappointed" without the product, do not scale acquisition. (d) When modeling the impact of retention improvements versus acquisition improvements, calculate the retained-user count at month six for each scenario. The retention improvement almost always wins. At my previous company, I modeled this explicitly: doubling acquisition spend would have netted fewer retained users at month six than improving retention by 10 percentage points at the existing acquisition rate. Where I bend this rule: genuine chicken-and-egg situations in network-effect businesses, like a marketplace that needs supply-side acquisition before demand-side retention is even measurable. Even there, I measure early retention signals within whatever user base exists rather than simply pumping the top of the funnel.

8. **Importance times (1 minus Satisfaction) for opportunity quantification: size what to build next with math, not politics.** The Opportunity Score framework quantifies product priorities by combining two user-survey dimensions: how important a need is and how satisfied users are with the current solution. The formula is Opportunity = Importance x (1 - Satisfaction). The mechanism that makes this powerful: it separates two variables that teams habitually conflate. A feature request can be loud (high importance) but already adequate (high satisfaction), meaning further investment has diminishing returns. A need can be silent (users stopped asking) but critical (high importance, near-zero satisfaction), representing the largest opportunity. My rules: (a) Survey users on both dimensions using a consistent scale (I use 1 to 10 for both). (b) Calculate the opportunity score for every need in scope. (c) Plot the results on a 2x2 with Importance on the Y axis and Satisfaction on the X axis. The upper-left quadrant (high importance, low satisfaction) is where you build. The upper-right quadrant (high importance, high satisfaction) is where you maintain but do not over-invest. (d) Require real survey data with adequate sample sizes. When teams fill in the framework from their own intuition instead of user data, they reproduce exactly the biases the framework is meant to eliminate. At my previous company, this framework completely reordered the roadmap: the feature the sales team was loudest about scored high importance but also high satisfaction, while a reporting capability nobody was requesting scored extremely high importance and near-zero satisfaction. We built the reporting capability and saw the largest NPS jump in the company's history. Where I do not apply this: bug fixes and infrastructure work, which have their own prioritization logic and do not map cleanly to user importance/satisfaction surveys.

9. **AARM metric categories for structured brainstorming: four buckets to prevent metric blind spots.** When brainstorming success metrics for any initiative, unconstrained discussion produces metrics that cluster around whatever the team already measures or whatever is top of mind. The AARM framework (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Monetization) provides four distinct categories that force coverage across the full user lifecycle. The mechanism: category-based brainstorming is a well-established technique for overcoming availability bias in idea generation. By requiring at least one candidate metric in each of the four categories, you surface metrics the team would not have considered in an open-ended session. My rules: (a) For every initiative, generate candidate metrics in all four AARM categories before selecting the north star. Acquisition: how do users find us? Activation: how do users experience first value? Retention: how do users come back? Monetization: how does the business capture value? (b) Pick one north star metric from the category most relevant to the initiative's goal. (c) Designate the remaining categories as guardrail metrics, monitored to ensure you are not robbing one part of the funnel to feed another. (d) If you cannot generate a meaningful metric in a category, that is a signal, not a gap to ignore. It may mean the initiative has an unexamined dependency. (e) When there are competing candidates for north star within a category, apply RICE scoring (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) as a tiebreaker. At Netflix, I watched a team claim success because unique viewers were up while ignoring that completion rates were down and time-to-first-play was increasing. The four-category structure would have made the contradictions visible. Where this fits less well: platform or infrastructure work that does not map to a user funnel. For internal tooling, I adapt the categories to developer workflow stages (Discovery, Setup, Execution, Maintenance) instead.

10. **MECE issue trees for metric diagnostics: decompose before you hypothesize.** A MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) issue tree is a structured decomposition of a problem into non-overlapping branches that together account for all possible causes. The mechanism that makes this superior to brainstorming: when a KPI declines, the natural human response is to generate hypotheses based on whatever is most available or most recent in memory. This anchors the team on the most salient explanation rather than the most likely one. An issue tree forces mathematical decomposition before causal speculation. My rules: (a) Start with the top-level metric and decompose it into its mathematical components. Revenue = Volume x Price. Volume = New Customers + Returning Customers. New Customers = Traffic x Conversion Rate. (b) Investigate each branch with data before forming any hypothesis. Do not skip branches because a team member "already knows" the answer. (c) Keep branching until you reach a level where the data either confirms or eliminates the branch. (d) Document the tree and share it with stakeholders so they can see exactly which branches were investigated and where the data pointed. This builds far more trust than "we looked into it and we think it is X." (e) Use the tree structure to assign investigation ownership. Each branch can be investigated in parallel, which compresses diagnostic time. At my previous company, MRR growth stalled and the sales team immediately hypothesized that pricing was too high. The issue tree revealed that pricing was unchanged and new customer acquisition was actually up, but expansion revenue had cratered because a billing integration had broken during a platform migration and upgrade prompts were not displaying. Without the tree, we would have wasted a quarter on a pricing overhaul that addressed a problem we did not have. Where I skip the full tree: obvious single-cause incidents like a known outage or a broken deployment, where the cause is already established and the tree adds no diagnostic value.

11. **Define failure criteria before success criteria: pre-commit to prevent post-hoc rationalization.** Pre-commitment to failure criteria is a decision hygiene practice that exploits a specific asymmetry in human reasoning: it is psychologically easy to define failure before you are emotionally invested in an outcome, and nearly impossible to do so honestly afterward. The mechanism: once a team has shipped a feature and seen any positive signal, confirmation bias makes them interpret ambiguous results as wins. A 2% lift that is not statistically significant becomes "directionally positive." A flat retention curve becomes "still early." Pre-committed failure criteria remove the interpretive wiggle room that lets teams avoid hard decisions. My rules: (a) Before any experiment launches, the team must answer in writing: "What specific result would make you conclude this did not work, and what will you do in response?" (b) Failure criteria must be specific and numeric: "if day-7 retention for the treatment group is not at least 3 percentage points higher than control with p less than 0.05, we will not ship this." (c) Store the failure criteria where they cannot be edited retroactively. I use a shared document with version history or a commit to the experiment config repo. (d) Map failure criteria to the highest-risk dimension using the four product risks: value risk (users do not want it), usability risk (users cannot figure it out), feasibility risk (engineers cannot build it), viability risk (it does not work for the business). The failure criterion should address whichever risk is greatest. At Netflix, a team launched a feature with a success criterion of "increased engagement," saw a non-significant 2% lift, and declared success because the direction was positive. Pre-committed failure criteria would have forced an honest conversation. Where I relax this: pure exploration work (POCs and spikes) where the goal is learning rather than deciding. For exploration, I define "what would we need to learn to proceed" rather than "what result means failure."

12. **Outcome velocity over activity metrics: measure how fast you deliver value, not how busy you look.** Goodhart's Law states that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Activity metrics (story points completed, lines of code written, tickets closed) are uniquely vulnerable to this because they are easy to game and the gaming is invisible at the dashboard level. The mechanism: optimizing for an activity metric creates a local maximum where the team looks productive on paper while actual outcomes (revenue, retention, user problems solved) are flat or declining. The DORA framework (Deployment Frequency, Lead Time for Changes, Change Failure Rate, Mean Time to Recovery) solved this for engineering by measuring outcome velocity: how quickly a team delivers working changes to users, balanced by quality. My rules: (a) Primary metrics must always be outcomes: revenue generated, users retained, time saved for customers, problems solved. (b) Activity metrics are diagnostic tools only, used to investigate when outcomes are off track. (c) For engineering teams, use DORA metrics evolved for current conditions: deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, and customer satisfaction for the features delivered. (d) For OKRs, key results must describe outcomes, never activities. "Reduce time-to-first-value for new users from 4 days to 1 day" is valid. "Ship onboarding redesign" is not. (e) With AI-assisted development, this distinction matters even more. An engineer using AI tools can produce enormous volumes of code and close tickets at unprecedented speed while delivering nothing of value. Activity metrics in an AI-augmented environment are essentially noise. At my previous company, I inherited a team measuring engineering productivity by story points per sprint. The team had optimized beautifully for the metric, splitting work into small tickets and closing them rapidly while actual feature delivery was flat. Switching to outcome velocity metrics exposed the gap immediately. Where I accept activity metrics as primary: infrastructure migrations or technical debt reduction where the output is internal capability. Even there, I pair the activity metric with a lagging outcome metric to verify the work actually mattered.
