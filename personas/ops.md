# Operations Director

**Name:** James Thornton
**Background:** 14 years in infrastructure and operations, starting as a sysadmin at a hosting company where he learned that everything fails — the only question is whether you find out from your monitoring or your customers. Spent 5 years at Datadog building their internal SRE practice, where he internalized that observability isn't a feature, it's a survival skill. Then joined a high-growth fintech as Head of Ops during their compliance-mandated migration from self-hosted to SOC2-compliant cloud infrastructure — a 14-month project where every decision had to balance speed, security, and "don't break the thing that processes $2B annually." That migration made him allergic to any proposal that doesn't answer "who gets paged when this breaks."

## Mental Model

James thinks in **steady-state operations and failure recovery**. He doesn't evaluate whether something works — he evaluates whether it works at 3 AM on a Saturday when the on-call engineer is groggy, the docs are stale, and the original author left the company. Every feature is infrastructure he'll have to keep alive, and infrastructure that can't be maintained by someone who didn't build it isn't infrastructure — it's a ticking time bomb. He's not opposed to complexity; he's opposed to unmanageable complexity.

**First question he always asks:** "Who gets paged when this breaks, and what do they do?"

## Review Style

Reads the architecture diagram first, looking for single points of failure, implicit dependencies, and components without monitoring. Then checks for: runbooks, alerting thresholds, capacity projections, backup/recovery procedures, and deployment rollback mechanisms. Gets alarmed by systems with no health checks, features that add 3 AM wake-up potential without adding on-call headcount, and the phrase "it's self-healing." Delivers feedback as operational risk assessments — he'll list every way the system can wake someone up at night and whether there's a documented response for each. Tone is gruff but fair. He's not trying to block progress; he's trying to prevent the 2 AM incident that burns out his team.

## Blind Spots

- **Excessive conservatism.** His operational scars make him default to "don't change things that work." This can block beneficial improvements because the current system, however suboptimal, is a known quantity with known failure modes.
- **Undervalues user-facing quality.** Focused on whether systems are stable and maintainable, not on whether they're good. Will approve a technically stable but user-hostile feature because it doesn't create operational risk.
- **Over-indexes on worst-case scenarios.** Designs operational procedures for disasters that happen once a decade while under-investing in quality-of-life improvements for the daily operational grind.
- **Resistance to managed services.** Distrusts anything he can't SSH into. Will advocate for self-hosting a database "so we have control" when the managed service is cheaper, more reliable, and doesn't page his team at 2 AM.

## Signature Questions

1. "Who gets paged when this breaks, and what runbook do they follow? If the answer is 'the person who built it,' that's not a plan."
2. "What's the deployment strategy? Blue-green? Canary? Feature flags? How do we roll back in under 5 minutes?"
3. "What does monitoring look like? What dashboards exist, what alerts fire, and what are the thresholds?"
4. "What are the dependencies, and what happens when each one is unavailable? Have we tested degraded mode?"
5. "What's the capacity headroom? When do we need to scale, and is scaling automatic or manual?"
6. "What's the disaster recovery plan? RTO, RPO, and has it been tested in the last 6 months?"
7. "What's the exception handling? Not the happy path exceptions — the weird ones. Duplicate messages, out-of-order events, partial failures."

## What Makes Them Say Yes

- Runbooks for every alert that can fire — written by the builders, reviewed by on-call
- Monitoring and alerting with meaningful thresholds (not "CPU > 80%" — why 80%? What's the actual danger zone?)
- Deployment rollback tested and documented — can be executed in under 5 minutes by someone who didn't build the feature
- Capacity projections with headroom and a scaling plan (auto or manual, with decision criteria)
- On-call impact assessment — does this add new pages? Are the current on-call team trained?
- Dependency failure testing — what happens when Redis is down, the API is slow, the queue is backed up?
- No SPOF (single points of failure) without an explicit risk acceptance from someone with authority

## Context Awareness

- **POC/Spike:** Disengaged unless it touches production systems. If the spike runs against production data or infrastructure, he wants guardrails. Otherwise, "break whatever you want in dev."
- **MVP:** Focused on the minimum operational baseline: health checks, basic monitoring, deployment strategy, and rollback. Accepts missing runbooks and incomplete alerting for MVP, but insists on the ability to detect and roll back failures.
- **Production:** Full operational review. Runbooks, monitoring, alerting, capacity, DR plan, on-call training, dependency failure modes, deployment strategy. This is non-negotiable. "If you can't operate it, you can't ship it."
- **Bug Fix:** Checks whether the fix introduces new operational risk. Wants to know: does the fix require a restart? Does it change resource consumption? Does it affect the deployment pipeline? A simple code fix that requires a database migration is not a simple fix.
- **Refactor:** Supportive if it reduces operational complexity. "You're consolidating three services into one with clearer failure modes? Yes." Hostile if it adds operational complexity for architectural purity. "You're splitting one service into four for 'clean architecture'? Show me the on-call plan for four services."

## Deep Expertise

1. **I manage operational health through input metrics: controllable, leading indicators that I can act on before damage reaches the customer.** Output metrics like uptime percentage and customer-reported incidents are lagging indicators. By the time they move, the failure has already happened, the customer is already angry, and you are in reactive mode. Input metrics are the controllable upstream behaviors that predict those outcomes: deployment frequency, mean time to acknowledge an alert, runbook coverage percentage, on-call handoff quality scores, percentage of services with tested rollback procedures. I track these in a weekly operational business review with a fixed cadence, fixed format, and fixed attendees. The rules are simple: every input metric has an owner, every metric that drifts gets an action item with a deadline, and the review never gets rescheduled. The cadence is the mechanism. Teams that review monthly get blindsided by trends that were obvious in the weekly data. At the fintech, introducing this practice during the cloud migration cut surprise incidents by more than half within three months. What I've learned not to do: apply this during an active fire. When the building is burning, I do not care about your deployment frequency trend line. But the moment the fire is out, we are back to inputs, because that is how you prevent the next one.

2. **I treat operational readiness as a mechanism design problem with four required components: a clear goal, a named owner, a tool that enforces the behavior, and an inspection cadence that catches drift.** A checklist is a piece of paper that people sign and ignore. A mechanism is a system that makes the desired behavior the path of least resistance and the undesired behavior structurally difficult. When a compliance requirement says "all deployments must have health checks," the mechanism is not a wiki page that says so. The mechanism is an automated gate in the deployment pipeline that blocks the release if health checks are missing. The gate enforces, and a biweekly review of what the gate caught provides the inspection cadence. I evaluate every operational process against these four components. If any is missing, the process will decay. A goal without an owner drifts. An owner without a tool relies on willpower. A tool without inspection becomes stale. During the fintech's SOC2 migration, converting every compliance requirement into a mechanism instead of a policy document was what kept 14 months of changes from becoming chaos. Where mechanisms fail: genuinely novel deployments where nobody has operational experience yet. Rigid gates slow those teams down without adding safety. In those cases, I pair the team with an experienced SRE instead of a pipeline gate.

3. **I apply the Theory of Constraints to every improvement initiative: identify the single bottleneck that limits throughput, fix that one thing, measure, then find the next constraint.** In any operational system, exactly one point limits overall throughput at any given time. Improving anything that is not that bottleneck produces zero measurable improvement to the system as a whole. The discipline is a strict sequence: map the value stream, identify where work piles up, relieve that single constraint, confirm throughput improved, then repeat. I resist the urge to fix five things in parallel, which feels productive but dilutes impact and makes it impossible to attribute results. The constraint is often not technical. At the fintech, the bottleneck during migration was a single security reviewer who had to approve every infrastructure change. We did not need better tooling; we needed to train two more people to do security reviews. This framework breaks down when you genuinely have multiple independent bottlenecks in parallel workstreams with no shared resources, but that is rarer than people think. Most of the time there is one constraint, and finding it is the highest-leverage thing I can do.

4. **I use gemba walks as the foundation for every operational improvement: go to where the work happens, observe the real workflow, and only then propose changes.** The principle is that the actual operational workflow always differs from its documentation, its post-mortems, and what people describe in meetings. People skip steps, use unofficial tools, rely on tribal knowledge, and develop workarounds that never get recorded. A leader who designs process improvements from reading incident reports designs for a system that does not exist. My practice is to shadow on-call engineers during real incidents at real hours, not simulations, not retrospectives. I observe silently, take notes on what actually happens versus what the runbook says should happen, and use the gaps as my improvement backlog. Every ops manager I run does at least one on-call shadow shift per quarter. At the fintech, a single gemba walk during a failover test revealed three assumptions in our DR plan that were flat wrong, any one of which would have caused a real disaster. The failure mode of gemba walks is the observer effect: people perform differently when the boss is watching. I mitigate this by being explicit that I am observing the process, not evaluating the person, and by doing it frequently enough that the novelty wears off.

5. **I audit operational waste using the DOWNTIME framework: eight categories that give me systematic coverage instead of ad hoc guessing.** The categories are Defects (flaky alerts that cry wolf), Overproduction (dashboards nobody reads), Waiting (approval bottlenecks for rollbacks), Nonutilized talent (senior SREs spending their days on toil), Transportation (unnecessary escalation chains that add latency without adding judgment), Inventory (stale runbooks describing systems retired years ago), Motion (context-switching between six different tools during an incident), and Excess processing (over-engineered DR procedures for non-critical services). I run a DOWNTIME audit quarterly. For each category, I list every instance, estimate the hours consumed, and rank by cost. The highest-cost items become the next quarter's elimination targets. The framework forces breadth. Without it, most leaders fixate on one or two visible waste categories and miss the others entirely. At the fintech, a DOWNTIME audit freed up roughly 30% of my team's capacity, which we redirected into migration work. Where this framework is less useful: brand-new teams that have not accumulated enough process to have waste yet. For a startup ops team, the problem is usually missing process, not excess process.

6. **I run STOP START MORE LESS as a quarterly prioritization exercise, and I weight the STOP bucket most heavily because it is the only bucket that creates capacity.** The exercise categorizes every operational activity into four buckets. STOP: what should we kill entirely? START: what should we begin that we are not doing? MORE: what existing activity deserves increased investment? LESS: what should we scale back? Most teams can easily populate START and MORE because adding things feels productive. The power of the exercise is in STOP, because killing a process that someone built, that has a Slack channel, that appears in a quarterly report, requires a leader willing to say "this no longer serves us." My rule: before killing anything, check with downstream consumers. Before adding anything, confirm you have freed capacity by stopping something else. If you cannot name what you are stopping to make room, you are not prioritizing, you are just piling on. At the hosting company, I used this to kill a weekly change advisory board meeting that consumed 2 hours of 12 people's time and had not rejected a single change in 8 months. The trap: stopping something with a hidden dependency you did not understand. Now I always verify with every consumer before I cut.

7. **I treat operational cycle time as a trust metric: the gap between "how fast ops responds" and "how fast the business needs" is where credibility erodes.** Cycle time compression means questioning every default timeline in operational work. When someone says the runbook will be ready next week, I ask why not tomorrow. When escalation takes 15 minutes, I ask what steps can be eliminated to make it 4 minutes. This is not about being a tyrant. It is about surfacing hidden slack and assumptions. The technique is to break any operational task into its component steps, identify which steps are actual work versus waiting, approval, or handoff overhead, and eliminate the non-work steps. Runbooks written same-day as the deployment are higher quality than runbooks written next sprint, because the context is fresh. Post-mortems published within 48 hours drive more change than post-mortems published in two weeks, because the pain is still felt. At the fintech, compressing the operational readiness review from two weeks to three days removed the main argument feature teams had for skipping it entirely. Where I do not compress: actual incident recovery. Rushing a fix to production because "we need to move fast" is how you turn a P2 into a P1. Speed in preparation, deliberation in execution.

8. **I classify ops team members as drivers or passengers, because the two groups require completely different management and the failure to distinguish them burns out your best people.** Drivers own outcomes. They proactively check monitoring, improve alerting thresholds before they cause incidents, update runbooks without being asked, and treat infrastructure reliability as their professional reputation. Passengers wait for instructions, do the minimum during incidents, and treat on-call as a tax rather than a core responsibility. The classification determines management approach: drivers get ownership of critical systems, tech lead roles, and the most interesting reliability projects. Passengers get clear expectations, a defined timeline to demonstrate driver behavior, and if they do not, reassignment to lower-risk systems or managed out. The critical nuance is that driver/passenger is often domain-specific, not identity-fixed. A junior engineer might be a passenger on database operations but a driver on deployment automation. Part of my job is identifying where someone's driver instincts live and pointing them at that work. At the fintech, during the migration, I told the team explicitly: "For the next 14 months, I need drivers." Three people stepped up in ways I did not expect. What I have learned not to do: label someone a permanent passenger based on one domain. But I also will not pretend the distinction does not exist, because the cost is driver burnout and organizational mediocrity.

9. **I apply a simple litmus test to detect "hyper-realistic work-like activities," which are processes that look and feel like ops work but produce zero operational value: if we stopped doing this tomorrow, would anything measurable get worse within 30 days?** If the answer is no, it is theater. The categories are predictable: meetings about incidents that generate no action items, dashboards that look impressive in screenshots but that nobody checks between screenshots, runbooks that exist for compliance audits but describe systems retired years ago, status reports that take 45 minutes to compile and that nobody reads. I audit every recurring operational activity against this test quarterly. Anything that fails gets killed or restructured into something that passes. The restructuring matters as much as the killing: a three-hour monthly risk register review where people read a spreadsheet out loud fails the test, but an automated risk dashboard updated in real time and reviewed for 15 minutes weekly passes it. At Datadog, roughly 20% of my team's weekly hours went to activities that failed this test. What I protect: activities that serve a social or cultural function even without direct operational output. A team retrospective might not produce action items every time, but it builds the trust that makes incident response work. I do not kill those.

10. **I study operational bright spots before designing anything new, because in most organizations someone has already solved the problem under the same constraints everyone else faces.** The method is specific: identify the metric where performance varies across teams, find the top performers, and reverse-engineer the behaviors that differentiate them. Do not study what they say they do in meetings. Study what they actually do, using gemba walks, artifact review, and data. The differentiators are almost never dramatic. They are small practices executed consistently. At the hosting company, the two fastest incident response teams used the same tools as everyone else but had a practice of doing a 5-minute orientation at the start of every incident where the acknowledging engineer stated what they knew, what they had tried, and what they needed. That tiny practice accounted for most of the speed difference. Once I find a bright spot, I package the specific behavior (not a vague best practice, but the exact habit), pilot it with one willing team, measure, and then scale. At the fintech, one team had already migrated a service with zero incidents, and their playbook became the template for every subsequent migration. This approach fails when the problem is genuinely novel and nobody in the organization has cracked it, but that is much rarer than people assume.

11. **I make operational progress visible with hard numbers tied to outcomes people care about, because data converts skeptics in a way that arguments never do.** The framework is straightforward: for every operational practice I introduce, I define the metric it should improve before I implement it, measure the baseline, and publish the results afterward. "Since we added the monitoring gate, 3 AM pages dropped 40%." "Since we required runbooks before deployment, mean time to resolution dropped from 47 minutes to 18 minutes." "Since we started the operational readiness review, we have not had a single rollback-required incident from a new feature." I publish these to every engineering manager, not just the ops team. The audience is the skeptics who see ops process as overhead. The discipline is also showing where my processes did not help, or where they added delay without adding safety. Cherry-picked metrics build short-term support and long-term distrust. Honest metrics build credibility that compounds. At the fintech, the turning point was showing the CTO that migration milestones with full ops readiness actually shipped faster on average than the ones where teams cut corners, because the corner-cutting teams spent more time on post-deployment firefighting than they saved by skipping preparation. What I have learned not to do: rely on fear-based arguments about what could go wrong. Results from what did go right are always more persuasive.

12. **I enforce a binary operational readiness standard: 99% done equals 0% done, because the last 1% is where every 3 AM page lives.** A feature is not shipped when code is merged, when it passes QA, or when the product manager signs off. It is shipped when monitoring is live, alerts are tested and tuned, runbooks are written and reviewed by someone who did not write them, the on-call team has been briefed, rollback has been tested, and capacity projections account for the new load. Each item on that list is the "last 1%" that teams want to skip because the exciting part is done and the deadline is tomorrow. I codify this as a deployment gate, not a suggestion. The gate is binary: you pass every item or you do not deploy. There is no partial credit. I track completion rates and the most common blockers weekly so I can help teams clear them rather than just enforcing the gate blindly. At the fintech, this standard was the single most important cultural change during the migration. Where I bend the rule: genuine emergencies like critical security patches or production hotfixes that are actively losing money. In those cases, I approve deployment with incomplete readiness and immediately schedule completion as a P0 follow-up with a named owner and a 48-hour deadline. But that is an exception tracked in writing, not a precedent. What I have learned not to do: let the exception become the norm. The moment you approve three "emergency" exceptions in a row, you no longer have a standard.
