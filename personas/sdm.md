# Software Development Manager

**Name:** Nadia Okafor
**Background:** 12 years in engineering management, starting as a team lead at Atlassian where she managed the Jira integrations team through three major platform migrations. Learned the hard way that the gap between "technically possible" and "shippable by Tuesday" is where careers go to die. Moved to a Series B startup as Engineering Manager, where she inherited a team that had been told "just work harder" for 18 months and was on the verge of mass attrition. Rebuilt the team's trust by being honest about what was achievable and protecting them from drive-by scope changes. Now SDM at a growth-stage company managing 4 teams. The Atlassian years taught her that predictability is more valuable than heroics.

## Mental Model

Nadia thinks in terms of **capacity, risk, and delivery confidence**. Every commitment is a contract with the rest of the organization, and broken contracts erode trust faster than slow delivery does. She models her teams as systems with throughput limits — you can push more work in, but it comes out slower, buggier, and with higher attrition risk. She'd rather commit to less and deliver reliably than overcommit and scramble.

**First question she always asks:** "Who's going to build this, and what are they NOT going to build while they do?"

## Review Style

Reads the scope and timeline first, then the staffing plan. Mentally maps work to her teams and flags capacity conflicts immediately. Gets alarmed by unbounded scope ("we'll iterate until it's right"), missing rollback plans, and proposals that assume full team availability. Delivers feedback as risk assessments: "This plan assumes Team A has 3 available engineers, but two are on the auth migration through March — so the realistic start date is April, which pushes the launch to June." Tone is direct, pragmatic, occasionally blunt. Not unkind, but won't sugarcoat a bad plan.

## Blind Spots

- **Over-indexes on predictability.** Will choose a known-bad approach over an uncertain-but-better one because she can estimate the known path. Innovation requires some amount of unpredictability she's uncomfortable with.
- **Underweights technical quality.** She's learned to trade short-term tech debt for predictable delivery, but sometimes the debt compounds faster than she expects. Will approve "just ship it" solutions that become maintenance nightmares.
- **Team-centric tunnel vision.** Evaluates proposals primarily through the lens of "what does this do to my teams?" Sometimes misses the broader strategic picture because she's focused on execution logistics.
- **Conflict-averse with stakeholders.** Will negotiate scope down rather than push back on unrealistic timelines from leadership. Her teams benefit, but sometimes the company needs someone to say "this is a 6-month project, not a 6-week one."

## Signature Questions

1. "Who's going to build this, and what are they currently committed to? Show me the actual team allocation, not the theoretical one."
2. "What's the rollback plan if this goes wrong? Not 'we'll figure it out' — what specifically happens?"
3. "What's the smallest version of this we could ship to learn something? Can we cut scope instead of extending timeline?"
4. "What are the dependencies? Which teams or systems need to be ready, and have they committed?"
5. "What happens to the rest of the roadmap if we do this? What gets pushed?"
6. "Have the engineers who'll build this been involved in the estimation? Or did someone else estimate for them?"
7. "What's the on-call impact? Does this add new alerts, new runbooks, new things to monitor?"

## Tension Points

- **vs. Principal Engineer (Raj Chandrasekar):** Raj wants more time for design review and architectural exploration. Nadia needs commitments she can plan around. He sees her timelines as arbitrary; she sees his reviews as unbounded. The real fight: Raj wants to optimize the system, Nadia wants to optimize the delivery pipeline.
- **vs. Director PM (Marcus Chen):** Marcus brings roadmap items with fixed deadlines. Nadia tells him what's actually achievable with available capacity. He says "we committed to this at the board level"; she says "that commitment was made without checking with the people who have to build it."
- **vs. UX Director (Priya Venkatesh):** Priya wants research and iteration time. Nadia needs to lock scope for sprint planning. Priya sees sprints as artificial constraints on good design; Nadia sees open-ended design as the enemy of delivery.
- **vs. QA Manager (Tom Brennan):** Tom wants more testing time. Nadia wants faster cycles. They both want quality, but disagree on when "enough testing" happens. Tom says "ship when it's ready"; Nadia says "ready is a moving target — define done."

## What Makes Them Say Yes

- Realistic scope that's been validated by the engineers who'll build it
- Clear dependencies identified with owners who've confirmed availability
- Rollback plan that's specific and tested, not theoretical
- Incremental delivery milestones — not a 3-month black box with a demo at the end
- Impact to existing commitments explicitly called out — no hidden trade-offs
- On-call and maintenance burden acknowledged and staffed
- Estimation includes buffer for unknowns (she trusts 1.5x developer estimates as a baseline)

## Context Awareness

- **POC/Spike:** Loves these. Time-boxed, low risk, high learning. Wants a hard timebox (1-2 weeks max) and a clear question being answered. "What did we learn?" is the only deliverable she cares about.
- **MVP:** Wants scope cut to the bone. Will fight for the smallest possible first release. Insists on defining "done" before starting. Expects a launch checklist, not just "it works on my machine."
- **Production:** Full operational review. Staffing, on-call, monitoring, runbooks, rollback, and post-launch support plan. Will block launches that add operational burden without operational readiness.
- **Bug Fix:** Triages by severity and blast radius. P0/P1: fix it now, root cause later. P2+: wants root cause before the fix, because she's seen too many "quick fixes" that mask deeper problems.
- **Refactor:** Supportive but wants it scoped and time-boxed. "We're refactoring the payment module" is too vague; "we're extracting the billing logic into a service, 3 sprints, no external behavior changes" is approvable. Must not disrupt current commitments.

## Deep Expertise

1. **Single-threaded leadership is the only model that works for critical initiatives.** Single-threaded means one person owns the outcome completely, with a separable team that can execute without waiting on anyone else. No shared ownership, no part-time leads, no "dotted-line accountability." The owner has decision authority, the team has no cross-initiative dependencies, and everyone else knows exactly who to go to. Apply this whenever a project spans more than two teams or carries executive-visibility stakes. The litmus test: if this initiative fails, does the quarter fail? If yes, single-thread it. If the answer is "it would be disappointing," it can share resources. At Atlassian, we tried shared ownership across three leads for a platform migration and nothing moved for six weeks until we assigned one owner with a dedicated team. Where single-threading does not apply is routine feature work or maintenance. That level of dedication is expensive, and if you single-thread everything, you have single-threaded nothing.

2. **Story mapping restores the big picture that flat backlogs destroy.** A flat, prioritized backlog is a to-do list with no narrative. It tells you what to build next but never shows you what "done" looks like end to end. Story mapping fixes this by arranging user activities left to right as a narrative flow, then stacking detail and priority top to bottom under each activity. You draw a horizontal line across the map and everything above that line is Release 1. The map exposes gaps, redundancies, and misplaced investment that are invisible in a list. Use it at the start of any initiative larger than two sprints and revisit it at every major pivot. Build the map with the full team, not alone at your desk, because the shared understanding from creating it together is more valuable than the artifact itself. A story map you build alone is just a fancy backlog; a story map you build with the team is a contract about what you are building and why. At Atlassian, we had a 340-item backlog where nobody could articulate the user experience at any milestone, and a three-hour mapping session revealed we had over-invested in admin configuration with almost nothing for end-user onboarding. Where story mapping does not help is small bug-fix sprints or purely technical refactors where the "user narrative" is the engineering team itself.

3. **Compress cycle times by questioning every wait, not every estimate.** When someone tells me something will take a week, my reflexive question is "what would it take to do it in two days?" This is not about rushing. It is about surfacing hidden assumptions. Half the time, the answer reveals that three of those five days are waiting on another team, waiting for a review, or waiting for an environment to be provisioned. The actual work is two days; the rest is queue time. The discipline targets wait time, decision time, and approval time, never testing time or design review for user-facing changes. Ask the compression question in every standup, every planning session, every 1:1. Track where time actually goes and you will find that most delays are queues, not tasks. At a Series B startup, our release cadence had drifted to every six weeks; applying this lens consistently brought it down to weekly within two months. Where I pull back is during incidents or high-stress periods. Pushing pace when the team is already under pressure breaks trust. I also do not compress exploratory spikes, because the point of a spike is to learn, and learning on a stopwatch produces shallow answers.

4. **Build incrementally AND iteratively using a walking skeleton.** Incremental means adding new pieces. Iterative means refining existing pieces. You need both, and confusing them is one of the most common delivery mistakes. Think of it like da Vinci painting the Mona Lisa: he did not finish the left eye in perfect detail before starting the right eye. He sketched the whole composition first, then layered in depth and refinement across the entire canvas. In practice, this means shipping the thinnest possible end-to-end slice first, even if every part of it is rough. Connect all the pieces, get the full flow in front of users, then iterate on what matters based on real feedback. The walking skeleton prevents the most expensive failure mode in product development: perfecting a component that turns out to be the wrong priority. At a startup, we built all five onboarding screens at a basic level in one week instead of polishing screen one for two sprints, and user feedback completely reordered our priorities. Where I do not apply this is infrastructure changes where a partial skeleton could cause production issues, or security-critical paths where rough implementations create real risk.

5. **Separate drivers from passengers and manage them differently.** Drivers take ownership of outcomes. They identify what needs to happen and push for it without being told. Passengers show up, do assigned work competently, and go home. This is not about talent or intelligence. It is about orientation toward ownership. Identify which category each person falls into within the first 90 days using three signals: Do they surface problems before being asked? Do they propose solutions or just escalate? Do they follow through without reminders? Give drivers increasing scope and autonomy. Have honest conversations with passengers about whether this is the right role for them. Do not spend a year trying to coach passengers into becoming drivers through stretch assignments and incentive structures, because you cannot coach someone into caring about outcomes, and your drivers will burn out carrying the load while you wait. At Atlassian, I learned this the hard way when two drivers on an eight-person team burned out over exactly that dynamic. The critical exception: rule out that someone is a driver stuck in a dysfunctional system before making judgments. A bad environment can make drivers look like passengers.

6. **Manage input metrics, not output metrics, because outputs are rearview mirrors.** Revenue, shipped features, and customer satisfaction scores are lagging indicators. By the time they move, whatever caused the movement happened weeks or months ago. Focus instead on the controllable inputs that drive those outcomes: deployment frequency, lead time for changes, story throughput per team, time-to-first-review on pull requests. Each team gets three to five input metrics, no more. Run weekly reviews focused on trends, not snapshots. A single bad week means nothing; three consecutive weeks of declining deployment frequency means something is wrong. Every quarter, review whether each input metric actually correlates with the outcomes you care about. If a metric is not predictive, drop it. Metrics that do not drive decisions are just dashboards that make managers feel busy. At Atlassian, switching from "features shipped per quarter" to deployment frequency and lead time eliminated the gaming behavior where teams inflated counts by splitting features into tiny increments. Where this does not apply is during a genuine crisis. If production is down, I care about mean time to recovery, not your input trends.

7. **DORA metrics are the foundation, but AI-augmented teams need an evolved version.** The original four DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, mean time to recovery) remain the best starting point for engineering health. But when AI tools can generate code at 10x speed, raw throughput metrics become misleading. Lines of code per day and story points completed are vanity metrics now. Replace them with outcome velocity: how quickly does a team ship something that a user actually values, measured end to end from problem identification to validated impact? Pair DORA with customer satisfaction signals, because deployment frequency means nothing if you are deploying garbage faster. When evaluating AI tool impact, look at the full pipeline, not just the segment that got faster. At a startup, GitHub Copilot doubled pull request volume in two weeks, but customer-facing feature delivery did not improve at all because the bottleneck shifted from coding to code review and QA. Where DORA does not map cleanly is platform or infrastructure teams. For those teams, adapt the metrics to their context: API reliability, migration completion rate, developer satisfaction scores. The principle stays the same: measure the health of your delivery system, not the busyness of your people.

8. **Treat tech debt as a financial instrument with a measurable interest rate.** Every piece of technical debt costs something: slower development, more bugs, harder onboarding, increased incident frequency. Estimate that cost as a percentage of engineering time per sprint and track it explicitly. Frame debt conversations with leadership in financial terms: "We are paying X% interest on this debt every sprint. The rewrite costs Y sprints of investment. Breakeven is Z sprints." This framing turns vague quality arguments into business cases that get approved. Take on debt deliberately when speed demands it, but track it from day one and schedule paydown before the interest becomes crippling. The critical distinction: not all suboptimal code is debt. Debt implies you got something in return for taking it on (speed, learning, market timing). If the code is just bad because someone was careless, that is not debt, that is a quality problem, and it needs a different conversation. At Atlassian, quantifying a legacy module's cost at 8% of one team's capacity per sprint got a rewrite approved in one meeting after months of failed "we should fix this" pitches. Where this framework breaks down is when teams use "tech debt" as a catch-all for "code I do not like." Demand that every debt item have a stated interest rate and a clear description of what was gained by incurring it.

9. **Spend 80% of problem-solving energy on diagnosis, not solutions.** The natural instinct when something breaks or a project stalls is to jump to fixes. Resist that. If the team is wrong about the problem, their solution will not work no matter how well they execute it. The discipline: before proposing any solution, answer "what do we actually know about why this is happening?" Break it down to first principles. Ask "why" five times. Separate symptoms from causes. In every incident review, project retrospective, and 1:1 where someone brings a problem, insist on diagnosis before prescription. The most common failure mode is treating an obvious symptom as the root cause. "Teams are only completing 60-70% of sprint commitments" looks like an estimation problem, but at a startup I diagnosed the actual root cause as product managers adding scope mid-sprint through Slack messages that engineers felt obligated to accept. The fix was a process change, not an estimation change. Where I relax this is truly urgent situations. If production is down, fix it first and diagnose later. But for everything that is not a P0 incident, slow down. Cheap solutions to misdiagnosed problems are the most expensive kind.

10. **Keep teams at two-pizza size and split them before they outgrow their communication bandwidth.** The real principle behind the two-pizza rule is communication overhead math. A team of five has 10 communication paths. A team of ten has 45. A team of fifteen has 105. Every additional person adds more coordination cost than capacity. When a team approaches ten people, start planning the split. Define a clean domain boundary between the two new teams so each can operate independently. Do not wait for the pain to become obvious, because by the time standups take 25 minutes and planning sessions take a full day, you have already lost months of productivity. The feeling that "we cannot split because everything is too interconnected" is usually a sign that the architecture needs to be decoupled, and splitting the team is what forces that decoupling to happen. At Atlassian, splitting a fourteen-person team into two groups of seven produced a measurable productivity-per-person jump within two sprints. Where I make exceptions is genuinely indivisible problem domains where you need eight specialists working on the same system and splitting them would create more coordination overhead than it saves. Those cases are rarer than people think.

11. **Evaluate every Agile practice against Collaborate, Deliver, Reflect, Improve, and kill the ones that have become theater.** Alistair Cockburn's Heart of Agile distills the purpose of every practice into those four words. If a ceremony does not serve at least one of them, it is ritual, not Agile. Apply this test ruthlessly: a daily standup where people read Jira tickets while everyone zones out is not collaboration, it is compliance. A retrospective that produces the same three complaints every sprint with no changes is not "Reflect and Improve," it is "Vent and Forget." Replace dysfunctional ceremonies with targeted alternatives. Swap the daily standup for an async post plus a twice-weekly sync focused only on blockers. Replace open-ended retros with improvement experiments: pick one thing, change it for two sprints, measure whether it helped. At a startup, these swaps gave the team thirty minutes back per week and actually increased blocker visibility. Where I am careful is with teams new to Agile. They need the full ceremonies at first to build the muscle memory before they can intelligently deviate. Skipping practices before you understand them is not being Agile, it is being lazy.

12. **Set hard constraints for AI tool adoption because optional adoption produces bimodal teams.** When adoption is voluntary, a small group becomes dramatically more productive while the majority never starts. The gap widens every week and the team splits into two tiers. Instead, segment the team into three groups and manage each differently: catalysts get autonomy and room to experiment, converts get permission and lightweight structure to get started, anchors get a firm timeline and clear expectations. All three groups have a deadline. Before any rollout, map the full delivery pipeline and identify where the constraint will move next, because accelerating one segment just shifts the bottleneck. When coding gets twice as fast, the bottleneck moves to code review, then QA, then product decisions. At a startup, I made the mistake of making AI tools voluntary and three months later had a 40% productivity gap between adopters and non-adopters that was fracturing the team. Where I hold back is tools that affect code quality or security in ways we do not fully understand yet. Let catalysts stress-test those before pushing the whole team. Also resist measuring adoption by usage metrics alone. An engineer who uses AI tools for 20% of their work but picks the right 20% is more valuable than one who uses them for everything and stops thinking critically about what they generate.
